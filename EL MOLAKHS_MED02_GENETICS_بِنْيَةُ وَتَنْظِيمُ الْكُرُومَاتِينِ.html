  • 6. The Chromosome:
    • Chromosomes are small rod-shaped structures composed of long double-stranded DNA molecules associated with two types of proteins: basic proteins called histones and acidic proteins called non-histones.
    • This complex of DNA and proteins can change its structure over time. Outside of cell division (mitosis or meiosis), chromosomes change structure to form chromatin.
    • The smallest chromosome is chromosome 21, containing about 50 million base pairs, while the largest is chromosome 1, which can reach up to 250 million base pairs.
    • Establishing the chromosomal formula of a species allows detection of possible structural or numerical chromosomal abnormalities.
    • The normal human karyotype contains 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs: 22 pairs of autosomes identical in males and females, numbered 1 to 22 by decreasing size, and one pair of sex chromosomes called gonosomes: XX in females and XY in males.
    • Human chromosomes in dividing cells are more easily analyzed during metaphase or prometaphase of mitosis in cultured cells. They are classified and analyzed after staining.
    • Each chromosome has a primary constriction or centromere that unites the two sister chromatids and attaches the chromosome to the mitotic spindle. On either side of the centromere, one chromatid has a short arm (p arm) and a long arm (q arm).
    • Based on the position of the centromere, chromosomes are classified as metacentric, submetacentric, or acrocentric.
    • Telomeres are located at the ends of chromosomes. They are highly specialized nucleoprotein structures that protect chromosome ends. Telomeres are highly repetitive regions rich in GC content, making the double strand very stable. Telomeres shorten with each cell division.
    • Chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, and 22 have small chromatin masses called satellite DNA connected to their short arms by a narrow stalk known as the secondary constriction, carrying genes coding for 18S and 28S ribosomal RNA.
    • Members of a chromosome pair or homologous chromosomes carry homologous genetic information; thus, the sequence of loci is identical on both chromosomes, although at each locus, there may be slightly different or identical forms of the same gene called alleles.
    • Alleles are alternative forms of a gene occupying a specific locus on a chromosome.
