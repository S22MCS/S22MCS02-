  • 3- Histological structure of arteries:
  • 3-1- General structure:
    • From the lumen to the outside, the wall consists of 3 concentric layers:
      • 3-1-a- The intima:
        • Composed of the inner endothelium and the subendothelial connective tissue.
        • The endothelium:
          • It is a simple squamous epithelium resting on a basement membrane.
          • Its cells are very flat and elongated in the direction of blood flow.
          • The cytoplasm contains fenestrations and Weibel-Palade bodies; sacs containing von Willebrand factor (factor VIII), indicating the secretory capacity of the cells.
          • The endothelium forms a selective barrier between the blood and the artery wall.
          • It participates in controlling contraction and proliferation of smooth muscle cells in the media layer.
        • The subendothelial layer:
          • It is connective tissue that varies structurally depending on the artery type.
      • 3-1-b- The media:
        • Usually the most developed layer in the artery wall.
        • Consists of smooth muscle cells and elastic tissue in varying proportions depending on the artery type.
      • 3-1-c- The adventitia:
        • Outer layer of loose connective tissue.
        • Contains small arteries and nerves called vasa vasorum and nervi vasorum.
    • 3-2- Structural variations:
      • A- Elastic arteries: conduction arteries (figure 3)
        • Large caliber arteries (> 7 mm), exposed to high pressure as they originate from the heart: aorta, pulmonary artery...
        • The intima: characterized by a thick subendothelial layer made of connective and modified muscle cells linked with a fine network and thin stacked elastic lamellae.
        • The internal elastic lamina is less distinct in elastic arteries; it is a thick, wavy, and discontinuous elastic layer.
        • The media:
          • The thickest layer.
          • Composed of 40 to 50 concentric and branched elastic lamellae associated with collagen fibers and smooth muscle cells with branching processes called ramified cells.
          • The abundance of elastic lamellae helps cushion blood pressure shocks and keeps the lumen open.
        • The adventitia:
          • Relatively thin layer made of fibrous connective tissue with longitudinal bundles.
          • Contains some longitudinal smooth muscle cells, vascular nerves, and vasa vasorum.
      • B- Muscular arteries: distribution arteries (figure 4)
        • Most numerous.
        • Include medium and small arteries (7 mm to 0.5 mm), such as the radial and femoral arteries.
        • The intima: thinner, composed of a subendothelial layer of connective fibers with an external wavy internal elastic lamina.
        • The media: varies in thickness according to diameter, made of several concentric layers of circular smooth muscle cells with some collagen and elastic fibers.
        • Defined internally and externally by elastic laminae.
        • Small muscular arteries (0.5 to 2 mm) have 3 to 10 layers of smooth muscle cells and only a thin internal elastic lamina.
        • The adventitia: may be as thick as the media and is made of bundles of collagenous and elastic fibers.
      • C- Transitional arteries:
        • Follow elastic arteries and have their characteristics plus those of muscular arteries.
        • Include:
          • Gradually changing transitional arteries: subclavian artery.
          • Suddenly changing transitional arteries: celiac trunk.
      • D- Arteriole:
        • Terminal arterial branches opening into the microcirculation, diameter less than 0.5 mm.
        • The intima: very thin subendothelial layer made of some connective fibers surrounded by a thin internal elastic lamina.
        • The media: thin, made of 1 to 3 layers of circular muscle cells.
        • The adventitia: very thin, grouping connective fibers, macrophages, and few nerve cells.
      • E- Atypical arteries:
        • Arteries that do not follow the general structure:
          • - Thin-walled arteries with few elastic fibers: such as intracranial arteries, cerebral arteries, and dura mater arteries, which have limited elasticity.
          • - Thin-walled arteries rich in elastic fibers: such as pulmonary arteries, which have limited contractility.
          • - Arteries with a blocking device (dispositif de bloc):
            • Smooth muscle cells in the wall form rings that control and close the vessel lumen, regulating blood flow (heart, kidney, lung arteries), involved in erection (penis, nipple) and menstrual cycle (uterine arteries).
            • Coronary arteries contain striated muscle of the cardiac type.
